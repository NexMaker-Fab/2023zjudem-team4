What is 3D printing? Reference: my3dconcepts
3D printing is an additive manufacturing process that builds a three-dimensional object from a digital file. An object is formed by adding material in layers based on the digital design in a computer controlled process. In other words, 3D printing refers to any computer controlled process that builds an object by adding material in layers following instructions from a digital file.
The History of 3D Printing
Reference: https://www.bcn3d.com/the-history-of-3d-printing-when-was-3d-printing-invented/

1980s

In the early 1980s, the first documented iterations of 3D printing emerged. Hideo Kodama in Japan developed a layer-by-layer approach for manufacturing using a photosensitive resin polymerized by UV light. Although he couldn't file a patent, he is often credited as the first inventor of this manufacturing system, which is an early version of the modern SLA (Stereolithography) machine. Around the same time, a trio of French researchers also sought to create a rapid prototyping machine using a laser to cure liquid monomers into solids. They were unable to patent the technology but are still credited with its development. In 1986, Charles Hull filed the first patent for Stereolithography (SLA) and went on to found the 3D Systems Corporation in 1988, releasing the first commercial SLA 3D printer. Other additive manufacturing processes explored during this time include Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) and Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM). SLS technology fused powders using a laser, while FDM involved directly extruding filament from a heated nozzle. These three technologies served as building blocks for the growth of 3D printing.

1990-2010

During the period of 1990-2010, there was significant growth in the field of 3D printing. In the 90s, numerous companies and startups emerged, experimenting with different additive manufacturing technologies. In 2006, the first commercially available Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) printer was introduced, revolutionizing on-demand manufacturing of industrial parts. CAD tools also became more accessible, allowing people to develop 3D models on their computers, which played a crucial role in the early stages of creating 3D prints. The machines during this time were different from today's, they were difficult to use, expensive, and required extensive post-processing. However, continuous innovations and refinements were taking place. In 2005, the concept of Open Source had a significant impact on 3D printing, giving more people access to the technology. The RepRap Project, initiated by Dr. Adrian Bowyer, introduced the idea of an open-source 3D printer that could build another 3D printer and various objects. The printing of the first prosthetic leg in 2008 propelled 3D printing into the spotlight. In 2009, the expiration of Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) patents from the 80s opened the door for innovation, leading to decreased prices and increased accessibility to 3D printers.

2010s

In the 2010s, 3D printers became more accessible to the general public due to declining prices. Alongside the lower costs, the quality and ease of printing also improved. The materials used in 3D printing have evolved, with a wide variety of plastics and filaments now available. Additionally, materials like Carbon Fiber and Glass Fiber can be 3D printed. Some creative individuals have even experimented with printing materials such as chocolate or pasta. In 2019, the world witnessed the completion of the largest functional 3D printed building. 3D printing has found consistent use in developing hearing aids and other healthcare applications. Many industries and sectors have adopted this technology into their everyday workflow.

New Materials

Reference: The Golden Age of 3D Printing: Innovations Changing the Industry | ArchDaily

 

Recycled Paper: 3D printing technology has evolved over the years, but concerns about sustainability remain. The majority of print material used is still plastic, which is not environmentally friendly. Designer Beer Holthuis has developed a method using wet paper fibers to create a more sustainable material for 3D printing. The Paper Pulp Printer is the world’s first 3D printer to use paper pulp instead of plastic or plastic-hybrid materials. The printed paper is strong and durable, and combined with a natural binding agent, the closed-loop system means the products can also be continuously recycled.

 

Steel: Designers in the Netherlands have used 3D printing to put familiar materials to work in new ways. The world’s first 3D-printed stainless steel bridge was unveiled at Dutch Design Week in October of 2018 by robotics company MX3D. The bridge was constructed by six-axis robots from layers of molten steel to showcase the potential applications of multi-axis 3D printing technology. The bridge also features smart sensors to track the structure’s performance in terms of strain, rotation, load, displacement, and vibration. The bridge is planned to be installed over one of Amsterdam’s oldest canals by mid-2019.

 

Ceramic : Researchers at Iowa State University are investigating the possible opportunities presented by 3D printing ceramics using the 3D Potter printer. They developed a 3D-printed ceramic facade that can be integrated with a building’s mechanical system to control light, airflow, and privacy, while also providing evaporative cooling benefits.

 

Food: 3D printing is leading to multiple new innovations in the culinary world. Company Universal Favourite has developed a 3D-printed mold in which to create their line of Complementary chocolates. The chocolates are molded so that two complementary flavors can be precisely fit together like puzzle pieces and eaten as one.

Neutrogena dabbles in 3D printing

On the skincare front, Neutrogena also unveiled its latest application of the technology: personalized 3D printed skin health supplements.

Neutrogena says users can create these ‘Skin360 SkinStacks’ using data collected via its digital skin assessment tools, which allow for the development of personalized ‘nutrient stacks.’ Once Skin360 app users have completed a self-scan, they can order their gummies from 3D printing specialist Nourished, each of which has nutrient layers designed to address a specific area of skin concern.

3D Printing Processes
Reference: The Free Beginner's Guide - 3D Printing Industry

Stereolithography

Stereolithography (SL), the first commercialized 3D printing process, is a laser-based process that works with photopolymer resins. The resin, held in a vat with a movable platform, hardens where the laser hits the surface, forming a solid object in a precise manner. The platform drops down a fraction after each layer is completed, and the process continues until the object is finished.

The SL process requires support structures for parts with overhangs or undercuts, which need to be manually removed. Many SL 3D printed objects also need to be cleaned and cured, with curing involving exposure to intense light to fully harden the resin.

SL is considered one of the most accurate 3D printing processes with an excellent surface finish. However, the post-processing steps required and the stability of the materials, which can become brittle over time, are limiting factors.

Image Source: The Free Beginner's Guide - 3D Printing Industry

DLP

Digital Light Processing (DLP) is a 3D printing process that works with photopolymers, similar to Stereolithography (SL). The key difference is the light source. DLP uses a conventional light source like an arc lamp with a liquid crystal display panel or a deformable mirror device (DMD), applied to the entire surface of the photopolymer resin vat in a single pass, making it generally faster than SL.

Like SL, DLP produces highly accurate parts with excellent resolution, but also requires support structures and post-curing. A notable advantage of DLP over SL is that it only requires a shallow vat of resin, resulting in less waste and lower running costs.

Image Source: The Free Beginner's Guide - 3D Printing Industry

Laser Sintering / Laser Melting

Laser sintering and laser melting are laser-based 3D printing processes that work with powdered materials. The laser traces across a powder bed of tightly compacted material, according to the 3D data fed to the machine, sintering or fusing the particles to each other to form a solid.

The build chamber is sealed to maintain a precise temperature during the process, specific to the melting point of the chosen powdered material. Once finished, the entire powder bed is removed from the machine and the excess powder is removed to leave the ‘printed’ parts. A key advantage of this process is that the powder bed serves as an in-process support structure for overhangs and undercuts, allowing for the creation of complex shapes.

However, due to the high temperatures required for laser sintering, cooling times can be considerable. Porosity has been an issue with this process, and while there have been improvements towards fully dense parts, some applications still require infiltration with another material to improve mechanical characteristics.

Laser sintering can process plastic and metal materials, although metal sintering requires a much higher powered laser and higher in-process temperatures. Parts produced with this process are much stronger than with SL or DLP, although the surface finish and accuracy are generally not as good.

Image Source: The Free Beginner's Guide - 3D Printing Industry

Extrusion / FDM / FFF

3D printing using the extrusion of thermoplastic material, known as Fused Deposition Modelling (FDM) or Freeform Fabrication (FFF), is the most common 3D printing process. Developed by Stratasys in the early 1990s, FDM is an industrial-grade 3D printing process, while FFF is a more basic form used by entry-level 3D printers.

The process works by melting plastic filament that is deposited, layer by layer, onto a build platform according to the 3D data supplied to the printer. Each layer hardens as it is deposited and bonds to the previous layer.

Stratasys has developed a range of proprietary industrial-grade materials for its FDM process suitable for some production applications. At the entry-level, materials are more limited, but the range is growing, with ABS and PLA being the most common.

The FDM/FFF processes require support structures for applications with overhanging geometries. For FDM, this involves a second, water-soluble material, allowing support structures to be easily washed away once the print is complete. Alternatively, breakaway support materials can be manually removed. As systems have evolved to incorporate dual extrusion heads, the issue of support structures has become less of a concern.

Image Source: The Free Beginner's Guide - 3D Printing Industry

Inkjet

 

Binder jetting and Material jetting are two 3D printing processes that utilize a jetting technique.

 

Binder Jetting: In this process, a binder is selectively sprayed into a powder bed of the part material to fuse it layer by layer and create the required part. Once a layer is completed, the powder bed drops incrementally and a roller or blade smoothes the powder over the surface of the bed for the next pass of the jet heads. Advantages include the negation of the need for supports as the powder bed itself provides this functionality, the ability to use a range of different materials including ceramics and food, and the ability to easily add a full color palette to the binder. However, the resulting parts are not as strong as with the sintering process and require post-processing to ensure durability.

 

Material Jetting: This process involves the selective jetting of the actual build materials (in liquid or molten state) through multiple jet heads, with others simultaneously jetting support materials. The materials are usually liquid photopolymers, which are cured with a pass of UV light as each layer is deposited. This process allows for the simultaneous deposition of a range of materials, meaning a single part can be produced from multiple materials with different characteristics and properties. Material jetting is a very precise 3D printing method, producing accurate parts with a very smooth finish.

Selective Deposition Lamination (SDL)

Selective Deposition Lamination (SDL) is a proprietary 3D printing process developed by Mcor Technologies. It builds parts layer by layer using standard copier paper. Each new layer is fixed to the previous layer using an adhesive, which is applied selectively according to the 3D data supplied to the machine. A higher density of adhesive is deposited in the area that will become the part, and a lower density of adhesive is applied in the surrounding area that will serve as the support.

After a new sheet of paper is fed into the 3D printer and placed on top of the selectively applied adhesive on the previous layer, the build plate is moved up to a heat plate and pressure is applied. This pressure ensures a positive bond between the two sheets of paper. The build plate then returns to the build height where an adjustable Tungsten carbide blade cuts one sheet of paper at a time, tracing the object outline to create the edges of the part. The process repeats until the part is complete.

SDL is one of the few 3D printing processes that can produce full-color 3D printed parts, using a CYMK color palette. The parts are standard paper, which require no post-processing, making them safe and eco-friendly. However, the process is not able to compete favorably with other 3D printing processes in the production of complex geometries and the build size is limited to the size of the feedstock.

Image Source: The Free Beginner's Guide - 3D Printing Industry

EBM

The Electron Beam Melting (EBM) 3D printing technique, developed by Swedish company Arcam, is a metal printing method similar to the Direct Metal Laser Sintering (DMLS) process. The key difference is the heat source, which is an electron beam rather than a laser, requiring the procedure to be carried out under vacuum conditions.

EBM can create fully-dense parts in a variety of metal alloys, including medical grade, making it particularly successful for production applications in the medical industry, especially for implants. Other high-tech sectors such as aerospace and automotive have also utilized EBM technology for manufacturing.
